Sagittarius A*

The center of the Milky Way galaxy, with the supermassive black hole Sagittarius A* (Sgr A*), located in the middle, is revealed in these images. As described in our press release, astronomers have used NASA's Chandra X-ray Observatory to take a major step in understanding why material around Sgr A* is extraordinarily faint in X-rays.

Messier 87

The elliptical galaxy M87 is the home of several trillion stars, a supermassive black hole and a family of roughly 15,000 globular star clusters. For comparison, our Milky Way galaxy contains only a few hundred billion stars and about 150 globular clusters. The monstrous M87 is the dominant member of the neighboring Virgo cluster of galaxies, which contains some 2,000 galaxies.

Cygnus X-1

Cygnus X-1 is one of the brightest X-ray sources seen from Earth. It was one of eight sources discovered during a sounding rocket flight in 1964. The rocket carried instruments to detect celestial sources of X-rays. The name "Cygnus X-1" tells us that the source is the brightest X-ray source within the Cygnus constellation. It us usually shortened to "Cyg X-1."
